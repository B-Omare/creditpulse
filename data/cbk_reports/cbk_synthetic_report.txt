CENTRAL BANK OF KENYA
FINANCIAL SECTOR STABILITY REPORT 2023

CHAPTER 1: CREDIT RISK IN THE KENYAN BANKING SECTOR

The Kenyan banking sector maintained overall stability in 2023 despite headwinds
from elevated inflation and exchange rate pressures. The ratio of gross non-performing
loans (NPLs) to gross loans stood at 14.8 percent as at December 2023, compared to
13.3 percent in December 2022.

Credit risk remained the most significant risk facing the banking sector. The increase
in NPLs was largely attributable to difficult operating conditions in key sectors
including trade, real estate, and manufacturing. Drought conditions in parts of the
country adversely affected agricultural lending portfolios.

CHAPTER 2: MOBILE MONEY AND DIGITAL CREDIT

Mobile money continued to deepen financial inclusion in Kenya. M-Pesa transaction
volumes grew by 18 percent in 2023, with over 67 million registered accounts.
Digital credit products disbursed through mobile platforms reached KES 850 billion,
representing a 23 percent increase from the previous year.

The Central Bank of Kenya notes that digital credit borrowers exhibit distinct
repayment behaviour compared to traditional borrowers. Mobile money transaction
frequency and balance stability are strong predictors of creditworthiness among
thin-file borrowers who lack formal credit histories.

CHAPTER 3: REGULATORY FRAMEWORK FOR CREDIT RISK

Under the Banking Act and CBK Prudential Guidelines, all licensed institutions are
required to classify loans into the following categories:

Stage 1: Performing loans with no significant increase in credit risk since origination.
Expected credit losses are measured on a 12-month basis.

Stage 2: Underperforming loans with significant increase in credit risk. Expected
credit losses are measured on a lifetime basis.

Stage 3: Non-performing loans that are credit-impaired. Full lifetime expected
credit losses must be provisioned.

This classification aligns with International Financial Reporting Standard 9 (IFRS 9)
which became effective for Kenyan banks in January 2018.

CHAPTER 4: CONSUMER PROTECTION IN DIGITAL LENDING

The Central Bank of Kenya Digital Credit Providers Regulations 2022 require all
digital lenders to disclose the total cost of credit, including all fees and charges,
before loan disbursement. Lenders must provide borrowers with a clear explanation
of the factors used in credit scoring decisions.

Borrowers have the right to access their credit information and to dispute inaccurate
entries in credit reference bureau records. Digital lenders are prohibited from using
discriminatory variables in credit scoring algorithms.

CHAPTER 5: CAUSAL DRIVERS OF DEFAULT IN EAST AFRICAN MARKETS

Research conducted in partnership with FSD Africa identified the following causal
drivers of credit default in East African digital lending markets:

1. Income volatility: Borrowers with irregular income streams show 2.3 times higher
default rates than salaried employees, after controlling for loan size and tenure.

2. Loan stacking: Borrowers with three or more concurrent digital loans have a
significantly elevated probability of default within 90 days.

3. Macro shocks: Drought and inflation events cause measurable increases in default
rates among agricultural and informal sector borrowers, with effects persisting for
6 to 12 months post-shock.

4. M-Pesa balance trends: A declining 30-day average M-Pesa balance is a leading
indicator of impending default, preceding missed payments by an average of 21 days.

CHAPTER 6: RECOMMENDATIONS FOR RESPONSIBLE LENDING

The Central Bank of Kenya recommends that digital lenders adopt the following
responsible lending practices:

- Conduct affordability assessments before disbursing credit
- Use alternative data including mobile money transaction history responsibly
- Provide plain-language explanations for all credit decisions
- Implement early warning systems to identify borrowers in financial distress
- Offer restructuring options to borrowers experiencing temporary income shocks
- Ensure credit scoring models are regularly audited for fairness and bias

Lenders that adopt causal AI approaches to credit risk assessment are better
positioned to distinguish between borrowers experiencing temporary shocks and
those with structural repayment challenges.
